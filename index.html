Volcano Metadata Schema

A metadata schema for describing volcanoes and notable volcanic events, with special attention to human, environmental, transportation, and climate impacts.

1. Opening Narrative

Volcanoes are documented in many scientific, governmental, educational, and historical information systems. Traditional volcano records often focus on physical characteristics such as name, location, volcano type, eruption history, and monitoring status. However, major volcanic events are also important social and environmental events. They can displace communities, disrupt transportation networks, damage infrastructure, affect public health, and influence regional or global climate systems.

This project develops a metadata schema for describing volcanoes and notable volcanic events from an impact-centered perspective. The schema is intended for students, researchers, educators, cultural heritage workers, and general users who need structured records about volcanoes and their effects. Instead of describing a volcano only as a geological object, this schema connects the volcano to a significant event and records the consequences of that event.

The schema was developed through several drafts. Earlier drafts identified the domain, selected example objects, and created a data dictionary. The final version implements the schema as an XML Schema Definition (XSD). During revision, Schema.org-inspired concepts such as location, start date, end date, and monitoring agency were implemented as locally defined elements in the volcano namespace to simplify validation. Dublin Core is used for general descriptive metadata, while project-specific elements are defined locally.

The larger design principle behind this schema is that metadata is not only a neutral description of objects. It also reflects decisions about what aspects of an object are worth recording. By emphasizing impacts, this schema makes volcanic events easier to compare across social, environmental, transportation, and climate dimensions.

2. Relevant Metadata Standards

Standard or Namespace Use in This Schema Reason for Selection
Dublin Core (dc) dc:title, dc:description Dublin Core provides widely recognized elements for basic resource description. It is appropriate for general descriptive fields such as the name of the volcano or event and a summary of its significance.
Local Volcano Namespace (volcano) Project-specific elements such as location, volcanoType, eventImpactType, humanImpact, and monitoringAgency The project requires specialized elements for volcanic events and their impacts. These elements are defined locally to make the XSD easier to validate and align with the project data dictionary.
Schema.org concepts Used as conceptual inspiration only, not directly imported into the XSD Elements such as location, start date, end date, and organization/agency are conceptually related to Schema.org, but direct implementation would add unnecessary complexity. Therefore, they are represented as local volcano elements.

Full Data Dictionary

Element Definition Controlled Vocabulary Example
dc:title The official or commonly used name of the volcano or volcanic event being described. None Mount Pinatubo
dc:description A brief textual summary of the volcano, its notable event, and its social, environmental, or historical significance. None A volcano in the Philippines known for its 1991 eruption and global climate effects.
volcano:location The geographic place where the volcano or volcanic event is located. None Luzon, Philippines
volcano:startDate The date or time period when the notable volcanic event began. None 1991-06-15
volcano:endDate The date or time period when the notable volcanic event ended. None 1991-09, approximate
volcano:volcanoType The geological classification of the volcano based on its structure, formation, and eruption characteristics. Stratovolcano; Shield volcano; Caldera; Lava dome; Cinder cone; Volcanic island; Complex volcano Stratovolcano
volcano:notableEvent A major eruption or volcanic event associated with the volcano, selected because of its historical, social, environmental, or cultural importance. None 1991 Mount Pinatubo eruption
volcano:eventImpactType The major category or categories of impact caused by the volcanic event. Human casualty; Evacuation/displacement; Climate impact; Transportation disruption; Economic damage; Environmental impact; Cultural heritage impact; Urban destruction; Tsunami impact Climate impact
volcano:humanImpact The effects of the volcanic event on people and communities, including casualties, injuries, evacuation, displacement, health effects, or settlement damage. None Large-scale evacuation and displacement of nearby communities.
volcano:populationExposure The level of nearby population vulnerability or exposure to volcanic risk. Low; Moderate; High; Very High; Unknown High
volcano:climateEffect The effect of a volcanic event on regional or global climate systems, including atmospheric cooling, aerosol release, or ash-related atmospheric change. Not documented; Local atmospheric effect; Regional climate effect; Global cooling; Long-term climate influence; Unknown Global cooling
volcano:transportationDisruption Disruption to transportation systems caused by volcanic activity, including aviation, roads, railways, maritime routes, or access routes. Not documented; Aviation disruption; Road closure; Railway disruption; Maritime disruption; Multi-system disruption; Access restriction Aviation disruption
volcano:responseStrategy Emergency, monitoring, evacuation, mitigation, or recovery actions taken before, during, or after a volcanic event. Hazard monitoring; Evacuation order; Exclusion zone; Emergency shelter; Airspace regulation; Road closure; Long-term recovery; Public warning Evacuation order
volcano:monitoringAgency The agency, observatory, or institution responsible for monitoring volcanic activity, issuing warnings, or managing volcanic risk. None Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS)
Implementation note: In the XSD, these elements are represented without sub-elements. Repeated information, such as multiple impact types or response strategies, is represented by repeating the element.
